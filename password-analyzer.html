Password Security: Explained

Learn how attackers crack passwords and what actually makes one secure. Tap any question to expand.

Weak passwords are the leading cause of account takeovers. In 2023 alone, over 8 billion credentials were exposed in data breaches. Attackers automatically reuse those lists against every other service you use within minutes.

A strong password is your first and most reliable line of defence. Even if a company's database is fully breached, a high-entropy password would take centuries to crack, making it worthless to attackers. It matters most for email, banking, and any account used for account recovery.

Entropy measures unpredictability. In password security it's calculated as: Length × log&sub2;(character pool size). The more possible combinations, the higher the entropy and the harder the password is to crack.

Example: an 8-character lowercase password has only 26&sup8; ≈ 200 billion combinations (37 bits). Add uppercase, digits, and symbols and reach the 12-character mark to get 80+ bits, exponentially harder.

  • Under 40 bits: Crackable in under a day on modern hardware
  • 60–80 bits: Safe against most real-world attacks
  • 100+ bits: Military-grade, effectively uncrackable

Credential stuffing is when attackers take username/password pairs from one data breach and automatically try them against hundreds of other websites. Since most people reuse passwords, the success rate is typically 0.1% to 2% per attempt. Across billions of stolen credentials, that's millions of compromised accounts.

This means if your email and password leaked from a small forum, attackers will try the exact same combination on Gmail, PayPal, Amazon, and your bank — automatically, within minutes. The only defence is using a unique password for every site.

When you reuse a password, you create a single point of failure. The weakest site you've ever signed up for (the one that stored passwords in plaintext) holds the key to every account you applied that password to.

There are now over 10 billion unique credentials in publicly known breach databases. Attackers compile these into mega-lists like RockYou2021 and run them against live services in seconds. Your "secure" password only needs to leak from one place, once, to compromise everything you use it for.

Password managers store all your passwords in an encrypted vault protected by a single master password. The best ones use zero-knowledge encryption — the provider never sees your master password or vault contents. Only you can decrypt it.

They use AES-256 encryption derived from your master password via slow algorithms (PBKDF2, Argon2) making brute-forcing the master password impractical. Even if the company's servers are breached, the encrypted vault is useless without your master password.

  • Bitwarden: open-source, excellent free tier
  • 1Password: great UX, ideal for teams and families
  • KeePassXC: fully offline and open-source

Two-factor authentication adds a second verification step on top of your password: something you have (like your phone) in addition to something you know (your password). Even if an attacker steals your password, they still can't log in without that second factor.

Forms of 2FA, from weakest to strongest:

  • SMS codes: better than nothing, but vulnerable to SIM swapping
  • Authenticator apps (Authy, Google Authenticator): much more secure
  • Hardware security keys (YubiKey): gold standard, phishing-proof
  • Passkeys: the future: cryptographic, device-bound, no password needed

A passphrase is a sequence of random words, like coral-phantom-glacier-titan-spark. Popularised by the famous XKCD "correct horse battery staple" comic, a 5-word passphrase from a 2,048-word list provides 55+ bits of entropy, more than most complex 10-character passwords.

They're easier to type on mobile, easier to remember, and just as strong as (or stronger than) random strings. The key is that the words must be truly random: not a meaningful phrase like "I love my dog" (which is weak because attackers test natural language patterns). NIST SP 800-63B (updated 2024) is the US government's authoritative password guidance. Its key recommendations reversed many old "best practices":

  • ✓ Length over complexity: a 20-char password without symbols beats a 8-char one with symbols
  • ✓ No mandatory rotation: stop forcing regular changes; change only if breached
  • ✓ Check against breach lists: block passwords found in known data breaches
  • ✗ No mandatory complexity rules: forced uppercase + number + symbol just creates predictable patterns like "Password1!"
  • ✗ No knowledge-based security questions: "mother's maiden name" is insecure

When a database breach occurs, attackers steal a file containing millions of rows of usernames and hashed passwords. Hashing is a one-way operation: a strong hash (bcrypt, Argon2) can't be reversed directly.

However, attackers run dictionary attacks (testing millions of known passwords against the hash) and brute-force. If you used "password123", they crack it in milliseconds. A high-entropy random password might take millennia. After cracking what they can, the results get sold on dark web markets and merged into mega-databases like RockYou2021 (8.4 billion unique entries). That's exactly what HaveIBeenPwned indexes.

Modern password cracking uses several techniques, often combined:

  • Dictionary attack: Tests millions of known passwords and common words first. "password", "qwerty123" fall instantly.
  • Rule-based mutation: Applies transformations to dictionary words, capitalising first letter, append 1–999, substitute letters (@→a, 3→e). "Password123!" is cracked this way.
  • Brute force: Tries every combination. Feasible up to ~10 characters on MD5 with a GPU rig.
  • Rainbow tables: Pre-computed lookup tables. Defeated entirely by modern salted hashing (bcrypt, Argon2).
  • Credential stuffing: Tests previously leaked pairs from other breaches.

Tools like Hashcat on a single consumer GPU can test 10+ billion MD5 hashes per second. Short or common passwords have no chance.

The old advice to "change your password every 90 days" has been officially retired by NIST and most security experts. Research found that forced rotation weakens security — people make predictable changes: "Password1" → "Password2" → "Password3".

Current guidance: only change a password if there's a reason to: if you suspect it's compromised, if the service reports a breach, or if you've reused it somewhere that was breached. Instead, focus on making the initial password strong, unique, and backing it up with 2FA.

Three things make a password effectively uncrackable:

  • Length (most important): Each extra character multiplies the search space exponentially. A 20-char random password is astronomically harder than a 12-char one.
  • True randomness: Generated by a cryptographically secure random number generator, not chosen by a human brain, which is terrible at genuine randomness.
  • Uniqueness: Even a perfect 128-bit password is worthless if reused. It only needs to leak once.

In practice: a randomly generated 20-character password or a 6-word passphrase provides 100+ bits of entropy. Even a nation-state actor with dedicated hardware would need longer than the age of the universe to crack it by brute force alone.
